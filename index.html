TLDR: The career paths readily available to me were not compatible with my life goals.

Full Answer: When I chose to major in chemistry, I was following a passion without thought to the real practicalities of a career. In chemistry (and molecular biology) laboratories, you essentially pick a problem, build a system to analyze/fix/address the problem, then refine and/or adjust that system to produce as many papers/grants/products as possible. Functionally, this means an initial period of trouble-shooting, followed by years of performing essentially the same procedure with minor tweeks. But I love the problem-solving and while I can perform repetitive tasks (such as manual testing), it is not my preferred long-term career goal. So I started looking for a career path that would provide me with consistent new, interesting problems to solve.

TLDR: Because I enjoy and am good at it.

Full Answer: When I realized that continuing in a laboratory setting was not going to provide a practical and enjoyable career path for me, I began looking for alternatives. My husband has been in software QA for many years and suggested that, with my knowledge of programming, attention to detail, and problem-solving skills, software automation testing would be a good career to pursue. So I started learning both functional skills and general testing practices. I started with a comprehensive Udemy course on Java-based Selenium Webdriver and studied for the ISTQB foundation level certification. At that point, I knew that not only could I succeed in a software QA position, but that I would enjoy it. I have an appreciation for both the verification and validation aspects of the role and love attending to the details of a project while also understanding how everything fits together to achieve the overarching objectives. So I've continued building my skills and knowledge in pursuit of this career path.
