The two may seem disparate at first, but they are actually very similar, once you get past the fact that one deals with physical compounds using lab equipment and the other deals with code using computers.

The problem-solving process is remarkably similar among all scientific disciplines. Debugging code follows the same essential steps as trouble-shooting a science experiment. You analyze what went wrong, assess what part (or parts) of your code/experiment is most likely to cause that kind of issue, and investigate that area. You then implement a potential solution and analyze the results. Debugging can even be easier and faster because a science experiment doesn't usually come with an error log or call stack, you can't always analyze your results at intermediate steps, even if you could analyze an intermediary, it isn't always possible to run specific steps in isolation, and it almost always takes more time, effort, and materials to retry a science experiment compared to rerunning a validation test for a software bug.

Additionally, following the protocol for a scientific experiment is likewise very similar to performing a manual software test. It's not just doing the steps, it's also knowing the purpose of each step and the expected result. This allows you to assess when you have encountered an unexpected result, even in the middle of the test. You can then assess if it might be human error, a fluke/glitch, or an actual issue with the protocol. In manual testing, that issue might be with the test or the software, so a thorough understanding of what you are testing and how it's supposed to behave are essential. In the event of human error or a glitch, understanding the test/procedure can allow you to assess the risk/reward of trying to remediate the error, as well as how to do it.
